The Mythmaker

Chapter I — The Ghost

🏠 StoryForge

They called him many things in the cities along the eastern valleys — menace, butcher, phantom — but the name that endured was the one whispered by frightened recruits during night patrols:

The Ghost

For months now, the Ghost had haunted the nation’s borders, striking from abandoned rail yards and sun-bleached industrial ruins where even the wind avoided lingering. Twelve officers gone, scattered across lonely concrete clearings and empty service roads, each encounter ending the same way — with confusion, smoke, and silence.

No one knew where he came from. No one knew what he wanted. Only that he was a rebel — and in this nation, rebels were reminders of a long-buried past people no longer wished to remember

That was why the Executioner hunted him.

The Executioner — hero of eleven great purges, guardian of the single-continent nation, the one who kept civilization from rotting inward. The people spoke his name with devotion, like a vow. Cities thrived beneath his order. Streets gleamed. Families slept without fear because he went where others would not, because he pursued those who challenged peace.

And he was here tonight

Rain slipped over the abandoned freight complex in thin, silver strands, turning broken tiles into mirrors. Beyond the perimeter lights, rusted gates hunched over a cracked asphalt yard.

Inside the command vehicle, officers listened to the hiss of static.

Then his voice arrived — steady, patient, reassuring.

“Unit Seven, hold your position on the western flank,” the Executioner said over the radio. His tone carried a calm authority that steadied men who had seen too much. “He favors choke points. Don’t give him angles.”

“Yes, sir,” replied Captain Luren, breath tight with focus.

The Ghost had been sighted slipping through the yard half an hour earlier — a blur in a long coat, moving with deliberate caution. Traces on thermal scans. A shadow where there should have been nothing

The officers took their positions.

Between two crumbling warehouse walls, a line of police peered into darkness, their lights cutting pale cones through drifting mist.

“Movement!” someone shouted.

Rifles rose. Fingers tightened.

They fired

Bullets shattered a concrete wall, spraying dust across the yard. The echoes rolled through hollow buildings and came back like distant thunder.

“Cease,” the Executioner said calmly. “He’s baiting you. Focus on the doorway.”

The doorway

A black square in the ruined wall. Too still. Too patient

A faint metallic clink rolled across the stones.

Then something small bounced into the light.

“Scatter—!”

A hard flash bloomed white. Heat. Pressure. Dust roared outward in a single violent breath. Officers staggered back, some collapsing, others scrambling for cover as smoke swallowed faces and uniforms alike.

“Stay together!” Luren shouted hoarsely. “Hold formation!”

Through the haze came footsteps — quiet, measured, almost unhurried.

A figure stepped through the doorway like a shadow peeling free from darkness.

He did not shout. He did not hesitate. He moved as though he already knew where everyone stood. To some, he appeared barely there at all — the outline of a coat, a flicker of steel, a breath of motion.

“He’s not human—”

“He should be dead—”

Steel clashed in the mist. A rifle skidded across stone. A short, strained cry cut off too quickly.

The Ghost slipped between beams of light as though even the light refused to hold him.

“Hold your line!” the Executioner ordered. His voice did not waver. “Two units north corridor. Drive him inward. Discipline — not panic.”

They obeyed him. They always did.

For years, he had delivered order where none existed. He had shattered rebellions and sealed fractures in the nation’s heart. He had pulled civilization back from chaos.

He was the hero who hunted those who threatened peace.

“Sir, we have him pinned,” Luren said. “He’s retreating toward the central gate.”

“Good,” the Executioner replied. “Do not rush. He wants mistakes.”

The Ghost halted beneath the rusted archway of the freight gate.

Rain traced silver lines along his hooded silhouette. The yard lights flickered. The air grew still.

For a moment, no one moved.

The officers — battered, shaken, resolute — formed a crescent around him. Weeks of rumors and trails ended here. Tonight they would end the last echo of rebellion.

“You’ve done well,” the Executioner said in their ears. “Hold steady. This ends now.”

“Drop your weapon!” Luren shouted.

The Ghost tilted his head slightly, as if listening to a sound carried by the rain.

A faint tremor rippled through the ground.

Something shifted at the far end of the yard — smoke curling from another doorway, another shadow where there hadn’t been one a heartbeat ago.

“Sir— there’s movement—”

“Eyes front,” the Executioner warned. “He uses distraction.”

But as the smoke thinned, the figure beneath the gate stepped backward into darkness —

—and vanished.

No footprints. No echo. No trace but rain on broken stone.

“He’s gone,” Luren whispered.

Static filled the channel

Then the Executioner spoke, calm and certain

“Regroup. Secure the perimeter. We hunt again at first light.”

The officers gathered themselves — bruised, shaken, obedient — and the yard returned to silence.

Somewhere beyond the perimeter lights, beyond scanners and patrol routes, the Ghost slipped back into the unseen spaces of the continent.

The hunt was not over.

It never was.
