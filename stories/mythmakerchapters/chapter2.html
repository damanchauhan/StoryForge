The Mythmaker

Chapter II — Pride

Morning light rolled across the capital like a slow-moving tide, gilding marble roofs and glass towers with a pale, devotional glow. Flags hung from balconies and transit rails, stitched with the nation’s crest — a single continent crowned by a blazing sun — and the people walked beneath them with the quiet confidence of those who believed their world was safe.

Vendors opened metal shutters. Trams hummed along elevated rails. Children in school uniforms recited oaths about unity and vigilance as their teachers smiled and straightened collars.

On every corner screen, the same image cycled in calm, curated rhythm:

The Executioner standing against a darkened backdrop, coat lined in silver thread, gaze steady and sharp.

Aurelion Vex.

To the citizens, he was not merely a man — he was a boundary. The line between order and the chaos of the old world. The one who had silenced rebellions and stitched the continent into a single will.

In bakeries and stations and sunlit courtyards, people spoke softly of him

“He was there again last night,” murmured an elderly woman, folding her hands. “At the freight ruins. Protecting our boys.”

“My cousin serves under him,” a young mechanic said proudly. “Says Aurelion never sleeps. Says he carries the whole nation on his back.”

“They say the Ghost escaped,” someone whispered.

Silence followed for a moment.

Then a shrug — small, but faithful.

“If anyone can stop that thing,” a shopkeeper replied, “it’s him.”

By midday, the plazas filled.

The government square opened like a stone amphitheater, rising terraces packed with citizens in coats and uniforms, faces turned toward the grand podium beneath the high, white banners. Cameras hovered and clicked. The national seal glimmered above the stage — sun and continent, unity and vigilance — as a thin wind lifted the edges of the flags.

A murmur passed through the crowd when he appeared.

Aurelion Vex stepped onto the platform with the deliberate grace of someone who never wasted movement. His coat was dark, his posture composed, his expression neither warm nor cold — simply certain. The light caught the faint scar along his jaw like a signature written in flesh.

He paused, allowing the silence to gather.

The microphones bloomed open.

“My people,” he began.

His voice carried easily across the stone, deep and controlled, the kind of voice that made listeners straighten their backs without realizing they had done so.

“Last night, our forces engaged the rebel known as the Ghost.”

A ripple of tension moved through the square.

Aurelion bowed his head for the briefest second.

“Sadly,” he said, “the officers dispatched to contain him have fallen.”

The crowd exhaled, a single, aching breath. Some lowered their eyes. Some pressed hands to their chests. Screens across the capital gently dimmed their tones, framing the moment in solemn quiet.

“They died in service to this nation — to our families, our unity, our future,” Aurelion continued. “Their sacrifice will not be forgotten.”

Applause did not erupt. It swelled slowly — respectful, grieving, faithful.

He lifted his gaze again, and the tone of his words hardened like metal cooling.

“The Ghost is not a myth. He is not a rumor in the dark,” Aurelion said. “He is a vicious being who hunts the very pride of this nation — our soldiers, our protectors — without honor, without conscience.”

Whispers darted through the terraces.

“He has taken thousands,” Aurelion declared. “Officers. Civilians. Servants of order. He strikes from shadows and leaves grief in his wake. He believes fear will make us kneel.”

He leaned closer to the microphones, not raising his voice — only sharpening it.

“He is wrong.”

The crowd lifted their heads.

“We are a nation of one continent, one people, one will,” he said. “And as long as I draw breath, I will not allow this monster to carve doubt into our hearts.”

His name — Aurelion Vex — flickered across every public screen in clean, white text.

“I will implement a new task force,” he announced. “Elite. Specialized. Trained for this enemy alone. They will track him across every ruin, every border, every forgotten road. They will bring him into the light.”

Murmurs shifted into fervor.

“We will show the pride of this country to that creature,” Aurelion said, hand closing into a quiet fist atop the podium. “We are not afraid of him. We do not bow to ghosts. We endure — and we prevail.”

Applause surged — not wild, but reverent, united, like a tide drawn by a single moon.

Citizens wept. Citizens smiled. Citizens believed.

In the back rows, a boy lifted onto his father’s shoulders watched the man on the stage with wide, shining eyes — and imagined a world where monsters vanished simply because Renard Aurelion said they would.

Far above the square, banners snapped in the wind like echoes of a promise.

The press cameras closed in on Aurelion’s still, unwavering face

And across the nation, the story settled into place — clean, certain, unquestioned — like stone laid into the foundation of a monument that would never crack.

The applause ebbed as Aurelion Vex lifted one hand, calling the square back to silence.

“Today,” he said, “you will know the names of those who stand at the front of this hunt.”

A row of officers stepped onto the stage behind him — coats pressed, boots polished, shoulders squared beneath the weight of national pride. Cameras panned over their faces, capturing the shape of discipline and loyalty.

Aurelion gestured to the first.

“Unit One — Captain Isera Halvorn,” he announced. “Border recon veteran. Guardian of the northern passes.”

The crowd applauded — firm, confident.

“Unit Two — Captain Marius Calden. Streets Division. First into danger, last to retreat.”

More applause.

“Unit Three — Captain Yulei Raston. Urban counter-insurgency.”

“Unit Four — Captain Selka Varne. Industrial perimeter command.”

“Unit Five — Captain Orren Vale. Tactical pursuit.”

Each name echoed across screens and plazas like etchings carved into stone. Their reputations swelled larger with every cheer — soldiers made into symbols, men and women sculpted into pillars of public faith.

Then Aurelion paused.

“And finally,” he said, his voice softening not with weakness, but with pride,

“…Special Unit — Delta.”

The air shifted.

Even the cameras seemed to lean forward.

“This unit will operate where others cannot,” Aurelion continued. “They will be the spearpoint of our pursuit. And they will be led by my most brilliant protégé.”

The crowd murmured in anticipation.

He stepped aside.

A young man moved into the light

Tall. Composed. The edges of his uniform traced with the silver-mark of elite command. His eyes were sharp, disciplined — yet there was a quiet fire in them, the kind that came from being raised in a nation that believed in you before you were even fully formed.

“Arctan Valeon,” Aurelion declared. “My star student.”

The plaza erupted.

Children shouted his name. Old veterans saluted. People reached toward the screens as if they could touch his silhouette.

Arctan bowed his head slightly, letting the noise wash over him before lifting his gaze to the microphones.

“My fellow citizens,” he said, his voice steady, youthful yet unwavering. “I stand here not as a symbol — but as a shield.”

The square fell silent.

“I grew up in the world Aurelion Vex built,” he continued. “I walk streets he made safe. I learned discipline under his guidance. Everything I am — I owe to this nation and to the order that protects it.”

He did not shout.

He didn’t need to.

“The Ghost believes fear is strength,” Arctan said. “He believes he can hunt our protectors, strike our homes, and vanish like smoke.”

His jaw tightened.

“I promise you — he will not vanish from me.”

A wave of cheers rolled across the terraces.

“I will hunt him,” Arctan declared. “I will keep your children safe, your homes safe, your pride safe. We do not run from monsters. We confront them — together.”

Hands rose into the air. Flags swayed. Voices broke into chants of his name and of Aurelion’s title intertwined like prayer and anthem.

Aurelion Vex rested a hand on Arctan’s head — not theatrical, not exaggerated — a simple, unmistakably human gesture.

A teacher.

A father-figure.

A leader passing his legacy forward.

The crowd roared.

Screens across the nation froze on the image:

Arctan Valeon — head bowed in vow. Aurelion Vex — hand resting gently, proudly, upon him.

The image would replay for days.

The nation would remember it.


Elsewhere — Beyond the City Lights

The room was dim.

Not abandoned — lived-in, tense, purposeful. Cracked cement walls. Scattered crates. A flickering television balanced on stacked metal cases, bathing fifty or sixty gathered figures in pale blue light.

The broadcast played here, too.

But no one cheered.

Someone spat on the floor.

“Executioner pomp again,” a man muttered, jaw clenched. “Standing up there like a saint.”

“Hero of the continent,” another said bitterly. “Hero of graves, maybe.”

A woman crossed her arms tightly. “He’ll pay. One day, he’ll pay.”

Their reasons went unspoken

They didn’t need to be said.

Hate lived in silences more than speeches.

The footage cut to Arctan’s vow.

Some of the rebels scoffed. Some swore. Others looked away.

“He trains more soldiers,” someone said. “More dogs to hunt us.”

“He thinks he owns the world,” another growled. “Thinks he owns people.”

Murmurs rippled.

Then — quieter — a different tone crept in.

The conversation turned.

“…What about the Ghost?”

Silence

No one laughed now

“I heard he took out a whole border convoy,” one whispered.

“Someone said he was there last night,” another replied. “But nobody saw his face. Nobody ever does.”

A younger rebel shifted uneasily. “Has anyone actually met him?”

Heads shook.

“He’s not with us,” a woman said slowly. “He never comes to our meetings. Never sends messages. Never asks for help.”

“Good,” someone muttered — but there was no confidence in the word.

“He scares me,” another admitted under her breath.

No one argued

They stared at the dim television as the image of Arctan and Aurelion faded to the crest of the nation.

Two enemies in their minds.

One they’d sworn to hate.

One they weren’t sure was even human.

And somewhere beyond both myth and propaganda…

The Ghost remained unseen.

Watching none of them.

Answering to no one
