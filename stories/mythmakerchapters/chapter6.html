The Mythmaker

Chapter VI — Escape From Hell

By dawn, the sky over the capital was the color of steel — cold, hard, waiting.

Every screen in the nation flickered to life.

Aurelion Vex stood before the cameras, shoulders squared, coat still marked with dust and faint traces of battle — not weakness, but proof. The plaza below was filled with citizens who had not slept, who carried fear like a second heartbeat.

He did not raise his voice.

He didn’t need to.

“I do not fear a man who calls himself a monster,” Aurelion said, each word precise, unwavering. “If anything, he has made my work easier.”

The crowd listened in breath-held silence.

“He has shown himself to the world — reckless, cruel, rabid in his violence,” he continued. “And because he has abandoned shadows…”

His eyes hardened.

“…today will be the end of the myth of the Ghost.”

Applause swept the plaza — not wild, but desperate. A collective prayer disguised as certainty.

Across the nation, families clung to those words.

They wanted to believe him.

They needed to.

In the underground

The rebels gathered in a narrow, dim-lit tunnel room, maps spread across a cracked table, old radios buzzing softly with interference.

For once — arguments did not come easily.

“He declared war,” one man said. “Not just on them — on everyone.”

“He’s out of control,” a woman replied. “He doesn’t care who dies. Civilians, workers, children — all the same to him.”

“And yet,” another muttered, bitter and grief-torn, “they executed Torren… and none of us stopped it.”

Silence.

Not guilt.

Not yet.

Just heaviness.

“He said we could join him,” someone whispered. “Or be hunted too.”

Some flinched at that.

Some clenched their fists.

Some looked away.

“We are not his allies,” their coordinator finally said. “We never were. But the government will use him to crush us — to justify purges — to tighten their grip and call it safety.”

A murmur of grim agreement.

“So what do we do?”

The room breathed.

Then hardened.

“We burn the country thin,” the coordinator said quietly. “We stretch their forces until they snap. We strike everywhere. Bases, fuel lines, power grids — not to slaughter civilians… but to pull the military away from the people.”

“To send him a message,” another said softly.

“That we are not his enemies,” the coordinator finished. “But we will not kneel, either.”

Radios clicked alive.

Contacts answered from distant safehouses and hidden camps.

Across the map — ink circles flared into movement.

Operations began.

Command center

Screens glowed in cold blue light.

Reports poured in.

Explosions.

Sabotage.

Fires.

Evacuations.

Arctan Valeon stood over the strategy table, knuckles white against the steel edge.

“Casualties?” he asked.

“Minimal so far,” an officer replied. “Most strikes are logistics — infrastructure hits. They’re avoiding population centers.”

Arctan’s jaw tightened.

“Keep it that way,” he ordered. “Redirect shields. Evacuate high-risk zones. No reckless pursuit — not this time.”

He would not bleed more men tonight.

He would not make their names part of another memorial wall.

Aurelion entered the room — silent as a closing door.

He rested a hand briefly on Arctan’s shoulder.

“Delta moves with us,” he said.

Arctan nodded.

“I’m ready.”

He wasn’t.

He went anyway.

The Hunt

They found the Ghost on the fractured outskirts — an abandoned district swallowed by concrete skeletons and shattered metal towers.

Delta moved through the ruins like a blade.

The Ghost was waiting.

Gunfire cracked through rusted halls.

Steel screamed.

Shadows collided with flesh.

Delta fought with discipline — precise, controlled — every motion trained under Aurelion’s doctrine. And still, one by one, they fell. Their breaths vanished against broken pillars and stained floors.

The Ghost didn’t walk through them like mist this time.

He stumbled.

He bled.

His movements were slower — heavier.

Each strike cost him something.

Each step drained him further.

But he did not stop.

He tore through them like a storm that refused to die even as it shredded itself apart.

Arctan fought beside Aurelion — silent, furious, relentless.

The Ghost lunged.

Aurelion countered.

Arctan drove him back.

The fight was no longer myth.

It was raw, human, desperate.

And brutal.

By the time the last echo faded —

only three figures still stood.

Arctan.

Aurelion.

The Ghost.

All bleeding.

All burning.

None surrendering.

The Ghost staggered.

Turned.

Ran.

He vanished into a collapsing office tower, footsteps echoing up the hollow stairwell.

Aurelion steadied his breathing.

Arctan followed.

Moonlight poured through a shattered window near the top floor, painting the cracked wall in pale silver.

The Ghost slumped into the corner — chest heaving, coat torn, blood trailing across the tiles.

Arctan raised his weapon.

He did not hesitate.

A shot shattered the Ghost’s leg.

He collapsed — teeth clenched, no sound escaping.

Another shot tore into his shoulder.

Click.

Empty.

Arctan tossed the gun aside.

They faced each other with nothing left but will and hatred.

He stepped forward.

The Ghost lunged.

Steel flashed.

Pain seared through Arctan’s leg — a knife buried deep.

He grabbed the blade — fingers slicing open, blood soaking his palm — and ripped it free, throwing it across the room.

Then he hit him.

Again.

And again.

And again.

Fists burned. Bones screamed.

Rage poured out like years cracking open at once.

“You killed them!” Arctan shouted — voice breaking into something half-human, half-grief. “My people— my men— my FAMILY—!”

The Ghost did not answer.

He only smiled faintly through blood.

As if violence meant nothing.

As if death was merely a language he spoke better than anyone else.

Arctan struck harder.

The world blurred into breath and impact and muffled cries swallowed by broken walls.

Then—

silence.

Dust drifted in the pale light.

Aurelion appeared through the doorway.

He watched.

He did not intervene.

He placed a hand on Arctan’s shoulder.

Slow.

Measured.

Final.

The hunt was over.

Days Later

The nation gathered again.

The plaza overflowed with faces — solemn, tear-lined, expectant.

Aurelion Vex stood beneath the great banners, coat black against the morning sky.

He spoke like a monument.

“Citizens of our great nation,” he said, “the threat of the Ghost…”

He paused — letting the weight settle like stone over the crowd.

“…is finally over.”

Gasps.

Cries.

Applause breaking through grief like sunlight through storm clouds.

“The monster who terrorized our people has been destroyed,” Aurelion continued. “But this victory came with great sacrifice.”

He lowered his gaze.

“My men. My brothers-in-arms. And…”

A breath trembled at the edge of his voice —

“…my beloved student.”

The plaza fell silent.

Some wept.

Some bowed.

Some whispered Arctan’s name as if it had become something holy.

“He fought bravely,” Aurelion said softly. “He gave everything so that you may live without fear.”

He lifted his head — iron returning to his eyes.

“The rebels still remain. Their threat still lingers. But hear me — as we defeated the Ghost… we will bring order to the rest.”

The crowd erupted.

Pride.

Relief.

Trust.

Belief.

The screens froze on Aurelion’s face — mournful, resolute, righteous.

A hero.

The only survivor.

The only voice left to tell the story.

And the nation cheered beneath the banners for the dawn they believed had come.

The Cornered Beast
