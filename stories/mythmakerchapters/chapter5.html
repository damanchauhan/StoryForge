The Mythmaker

Chapter V — The Chase

The city held a day of silence.

Flags lowered. Screens dimmed. Streets hushed beneath wreaths and memorial ribbons tied to lamp posts — names scrolling in slow white lines across public displays.

Unit One.

Unit Two.

Unit Three.

Unit Four.

Unit Five.

Arctan Valeon stood among the rows of empty helmets laid across the memorial platform, his hands locked behind his back, jaw trembling but unbroken. Wind brushed against his uniform, carrying the faint echoes of prayers and speeches being recited across the capital.

He didn’t cry.

He couldn’t.

He felt grief like iron welded to his ribs — a weight he could neither release nor set down.

He bowed his head.

I should have been there.

He had commanded from a distance.

He had watched them die through glass and screens.

He had listened to their last breaths as lines went silent.

Resolve crystallized behind his eyes.

He would not stand behind a console again.

He would not watch from safety.

He would hunt.

He didn’t request permission.

He didn’t wait for orders.

He gathered Delta at dawn — eyes hollow, uniforms sharp, spirits wound as tight as his own.

“We track him,” Arctan said, voice stripped of hesitation. “We don’t sleep. We don’t stop. Not until he can’t run anymore.”

Delta saluted.

Nobody questioned.

For days they combed alleys, warehouses, underground corridors and abandoned docks. They spoke to informants, shook down back-street mechanics, raided derelict corners of the old city where fear curled like roots.

Whispers always led them half a step too late.

A shadow here.

A footprint there.

A rumor of a man who never stayed.

Until they found the shop.

A small place buried between freight lots — quiet, half-forgotten, lantern burned down to dust.

Arctan stepped inside first.

The merchant froze when he saw the uniforms — the emblem, the silver trim, the kind of authority that didn’t need words.

His hands trembled.

He didn’t ask why they were there.

He already knew.

Arctan placed a photograph on the counter — a blurred shot taken from an old surveillance lens.

A hooded figure.

A shadow.

The Ghost.

“You know him,” Arctan said.

Not a question.

The merchant swallowed.

His eyes lowered.

“…He’s been here,” he admitted softly. “A few days back. Hurt. Bleeding. I patched him up.”

Arctan’s voice tightened.

“Where did he go?”

“I don’t know.”

“Where did he go?”

The words hit harder the second time — not shouted, but sharpened.

“I don’t know,” the merchant repeated, voice cracking. “He never tells me. He never stays. He comes and goes like— like there’s nothing in the world that can hold him still.”

Arctan’s fist hit the counter.

The lantern rattled.

“You helped him,” Arctan said. “You gave him shelter. You treated him.”

The merchant flinched — fear shaking through his shoulders.

“He’d have died out there,” he said. “And— and I’ve known him for years. Before he— became what he is.”

Arctan’s eyes darkened.

“You still helped him.”

Silence stretched.

The room felt smaller.

Delta officers watched from the doorway, breaths thin, weight thick in the air.

The merchant tried to step back.

“Listen… I never told him anything. I never—”

Arctan grabbed him by the collar — slammed him against the wall.

“You kept him alive,” Arctan hissed. “You kept that monster breathing.”

The merchant winced.

“He… he wasn’t always—”

The words died under the impact of a strike.

Not furious.

Not wild.

Measured.

Deliberate.

“Where is he now?”

“I don’t know—”

Another blow.

Blood marked the floor in quiet drops.

Delta shifted uneasily — not daring to intervene.

Arctan’s voice grew quieter.

More dangerous.

“He killed my men,” he said. “He hunted them. Burned them. He slaughtered hundreds. You knew what he was — and you let him walk out alive.”

The merchant shook, breath ragged.

“I didn’t do it for him,” he whispered. “I did it because… because someone had to still remember how to be human.”

Arctan’s hand hovered — just for a moment.

A thin, fragile hesitation.

It vanished.

His expression hardened — steel sealing over wounds that had never healed.

“You chose your side,” he said.

The last sound the room heard

was not a shout

but a quiet, final breath.

By morning the city awoke to headlines.

ALLY OF THE GHOST IDENTIFIED — EXECUTED FOR TREASON

Screens filled with the image of the old man’s shop. Commentary rolled like righteous thunder — narrators praising decisive justice, anchors repeating his crime until it folded neatly into national memory.

“His death ensures the Ghost has no refuge,” one broadcast declared. “No haven. No sympathizers left.”

Citizens nodded along in cafés and transit lines.

“He sheltered a monster.”

“He deserved it.”

“At least the hero’s student is thorough.”

Some felt safer.

Some felt reassured.

Some felt nothing at all.

A few — very few — looked at the old man’s face and wondered who he had been before the word traitor swallowed him.

Their doubt never made it to air.

In the underground hideout, the rebels watched the same broadcast in silence.

No shouting.

No jeering.

Just the low hum of the television and the dull weight settling in their chests.

One woman covered her mouth.

“…That’s Torren,” she whispered. “The old shopkeeper from the freight district.”

A man sank into a chair.

“He used to fix our radios.”

“He gave food to people who couldn’t afford it.”

“He helped everyone.”

They didn’t know he knew the Ghost.

They didn’t know he ever touched that world at all.

But they knew he didn’t deserve the word printed across the screen.

They bowed their heads.

They mourned him quietly.

No flags.

No speeches.

No public ceremonies.

Just grief with no place to stand.

And somewhere beyond rebels, beyond citizens, beyond the screens that told the nation who deserved to live and who deserved to vanish—

A shadow moved through the city’s forgotten edges.

And when the Ghost learned what had happened,

the night itself seemed to hold its breath.

The city never slept — but that night, it watched.

Police patrols doubled. Floodlights turned alleys into harsh corridors of white. Drones hummed over rooftops in slow, searching arcs.

People whispered in bars and backstreets that the Ghost would hear.

People prayed he wouldn’t.

Somewhere in the gray and empty stretch between the industrial ring and the old financial quarter — a streetlight flickered.

Then went out.

A man locking his storefront door frowned and looked up.

He didn’t see the figure step out of the darkness behind him.

He didn’t even have time to scream.

Across the street, two officers patrolling the block heard the body hit pavement.

They turned.

They saw the hood.

The long coat.

The silence.

Their radios crackled.

Only one of them got a signal out before the air tore itself apart.

Gunfire burst and vanished again as quickly as breath on glass. The Ghost didn’t move like a man running or fighting — he moved like something that didn’t believe in hesitation. A blur. A strike. A shape that appeared where fear already was.

Someone shouted his name.

Someone begged to live.

He didn’t care.

He didn’t spare.

He did not choose his dead with mercy or message — he killed whoever stood in his way, whoever crossed his path, whoever happened to be beneath the streetlights when he passed beneath them.

Civilians stumbled from apartments in confusion — and never reached the stairwell.

An officer tried to pull a wounded partner to safety — and collapsed half a step later.

There was no pattern.

No restraint.

No meaning beyond destruction.

Blood spread across the pavement in uneven pools, catching the reflection of the flickering lamps like fragments of broken stars.

For the first time — the Ghost no longer lived in rumor or shadowed corners.

He stood in the open.

He made the city look at him.

He wanted it to.

Emergency broadcasts cut into every channel.

Sirens wailed across the capital.

A thousand cameras turned toward the disaster —

and then

without warning

a hijack override seized the screens.

The image twisted.

Static rippled.

And the Ghost’s face stared back at the nation.

Not fully lit.

Not fully seen.

But close enough to feel real.

Close enough to feel wrong.

His voice did not roar.

It cut.

“You will pay for this.”

The words rolled through homes, plazas, cafés, patrol vehicles, rebel hideouts.

Through the palace.

Through every place that had dared to call his name.

“You call me a monster,” he said — breath slow, venom coiled into every syllable — “then I will show you what a monster truly is.”

Behind him, sirens bled faintly into the audio — the city still burning at the edges of the night he had opened.

“This is a declaration of war.”

Some people froze.

Some fell to their knees.

Some whispered prayers.

His eyes turned — not to the people

but to those who hid behind uniforms and emblems.

“And rebels…”

The word lingered like a knife twisted with amusement.

“I never walked with you,” he said. “I never swore to your cause. I never called you allies.”

A few rebels watching the screen stiffened — breaths caught halfway between fear and reluctant fascination.

“But they took a man who still saw humans when the rest of you saw flags and enemies.”

His jaw tightened.

“And for that — I will tear their kingdom apart.”

He paused.

Not for effect.

For certainty.

“You can hide like you always do,” he said, voice sinking lower, colder. “Or you can hunt these bastards with me.”

He let the invitation linger.

Then poisoned it.

“But if you get in my way…”

His eyes sharpened.

“…I’m coming for you too.”

No one in that hideout spoke.

No one breathed too loudly.

The message wasn’t a plea.

It wasn’t unity.

It was a warning.

And finally — the Ghost turned his gaze to the last audience left.

The one name that every broadcast fed into myth.

“And you,” he said softly.

A hint of something darker curled beneath the tone.

A promise.

“Oh-so-great hero.”

His lips bent into a hollow, vicious shadow of a smile.

“Stop sending your dogs.”

A beat.

“Come here yourself.”

Another.

“Don’t run from me anymore.”

The broadcast cut.

Screens snapped back to chaos.

Police feeds.

Emergency lights.

Screams.

Static.

And then —

the rampage resumed.

He didn’t retreat back into shadow.

He carved through the city like a wound that refused to close.

Streets turned into battlefields without soldiers.

Storefront glass burst outward.

Sirens rose and died and rose again.

Every alley he crossed became a grave.

Every block he passed through became a memory no one wanted to keep.

The Ghost did not disappear.

He didn’t vanish with smoke or trick mirrors or impossible steps.

He stayed long enough to make people understand

that this wasn’t a haunting anymore.

It was a reckoning.

And the city — for the first time — realized

that the monster it had been told to fear

was far worse than the story.
