The Mythmaker

Chapter III — Killing Spree

🏠 StoryForge

The night on the quiet side of the city had the kind of stillness that made sound feel like a trespass. Old neon signs flickered above shuttered shops, their light trembling across rain-damp pavement.

Officer pushed open the door of a small convenience store, the bell above it chiming softly.

He just wanted a smoke.

The aisle smelled faintly of stale sugar and mop water. The hum of the refrigerator aisles buzzed like distant insects.

Then he saw it.

A uniformed officer sprawled across the tiled floor near the counter — throat slack, eyes open, badge cracked against the linoleum. His hand still clutched the radio, frozen mid-grip.

Officer’s breath went cold.

He drew his gun.

The store lights shattered.

Pop-pop-pop — glass rained down as darkness swallowed the room.

His heart hammered against his ribs.

“…Ghost…” he whispered into his radio, voice shaking, “Officer down— conveni— sto— I— need—”

A soft sound behind him.

Too close.

Something moved — fast.

The radio hit the floor.

Silence returned to the store like a closing mouth.

And somewhere in the shadows, a figure slipped out the back door and vanished into the night.

He ran.

Not because he was afraid.

Because survival meant distance — meant finding ground he understood.

He knew how the chase worked.

He knew where the killing would end.

He sprinted across empty lots and narrow alleys until he reached the broken shell of an abandoned factory district — rusted cranes looming like dead giants, shattered windows grinning black against the sky.

It was the safest place he could find.

The safest place to disappear.

The safest place to fight.

Sirens screamed to life across the city.

Screens flickered.

Broadcast lines opened.

Aurelion Vex’s voice cut through every speaker — unwavering, commanding.

“Unit One through Five — mobilize,” he said. “Target confirmed. The Ghost is active.”

Officers straightened in their vehicles, breath sharpened, fear swallowed.

Special channels lit.

“Special Unit — Delta,” Aurelion continued, his tone firm but measured, “stand by. Do not engage. Tonight your role is observation.”

A brief pause.

“I leave tactical relay to you, Delta-One,” he said — to Arctan Valeon.

The boy bowed his head over the console, jaw tight, eyes bright with a burning sense of duty.

“Yes, sir.”

His hands moved with precision across the tactical map.

“Units One to Five — converge on industrial sector perimeter,” Arctan commanded. “Choke lanes. No solo movement. Drive him inward — not outward.”

Engines roared through the night.

The hunt surged into the bones of the abandoned factory district.

The rain turned the broken concrete into slick, black glass.

Unit One entered first.

Footsteps echoed through empty catwalks and torn metal staircases. The air smelled of rust and old oil — and something colder, like a breath drawn in at the back of a throat.

“Thermals unstable,” someone muttered. “Can’t get a fix.”

“Hold formation,” Arctan ordered through the comms. “He’ll come to you.”

A can rolled across the floor.

Every rifle snapped toward the sound.

A shadow dropped from the scaffolding.

Three men fell before any of them finished turning.

Not screaming — not even understanding.

The Ghost moved like a fracture through the dark — there for a heartbeat, gone the next. A blur of coat, a glint of steel, the whisper of breath passing a cheek before silence replaced it.

Unit Two pushed in from the west.

“Push him! Don’t break line!” Arctan barked.

Gunfire cracked through the factory halls — stuttering, panicked, echoing off dead machinery.

A flash.

Smoke surged.

Someone shouted that he had him.

Then their voice cut off.

Unit Three found only blood stains where Unit Two had been.

“Eyes up!” Arctan shouted. “He doubles back — he always doubles back!”

A figure brushed the edge of a flashlight beam — then vanished.

Boots slipped.

Another man fell.

“He’s behind—!”

Silence swallowed the rest.

Unit Four tried to lock the north corridor.

They moved as Arctan commanded — disciplined, tight, overlapping lanes.

They did everything right.

It didn’t matter.

The Ghost did not fight them head-on.

He dismantled them.

Piece by piece.

One officer dragged himself across the floor, breath wet and shallow, reaching for his rifle — and it slid away from him into darkness.

He never saw who took it.

Unit Five arrived last — desperate, furious, terrified.

“Delta-One!” Captain Orren shouted. “He’s cutting through us like— like—”

Arctan’s voice hardened.

“Hold your ground. Reinforcements inbound. Do— not— scatter.”

They didn’t scatter.

They braced.

They stood shoulder to shoulder beneath the rusted girders, rifles raised, breaths syncing as one.

For a moment, it felt like courage.

Then the lights above them blew out in a shower of sparks.

A shape flashed through the dark.

Gunfire stuttered — wild — useless.

One officer turned and fired where a shadow had been — only to realize too late that he had fired into the back of his own teammate.

Panic cracked like glass.

And in that fracture — the Ghost moved.

Unit One fell.

Unit Two broke.

Unit Three went silent.

Unit Four screamed once.

Unit Five fought longest.

And then…

Silence.

Not all of them were dead.

Some crawled.

Some breathed.

Some still clutched rifles with trembling fingers and whispered prayers to names they had been taught to trust.

Arctan’s voice trembled at the edge of fury.

“All remaining units — retreat,” he ordered, swallowing the knot in his throat. “Get to your transports. Now.”

They staggered outside.

Into the rain.

Into the open.

Into the promise of survival.

Doors slammed.

Engines turned.

Wheels rolled.

For a brief, fragile heartbeat — they believed they were leaving.

Then the night flashed white.

One vehicle burst into flame — then another — then another — chain-fire ripping across the lot as if the ground itself detonated beneath them.

Metal shrieked.

Windows shattered outward.

The air filled with fire and shrapnel and unfinished breaths.

And the last survivors died before they ever cleared the perimeter.

Arctan stared at the screen.

Hands shaking.

Voice gone.

Fury burned into grief.

“He— he killed them— every single—”

His words collapsed.

Aurelion spoke softly through the comms.

“Delta-One,” he said, not unkindly, “this is how the Ghost works.”

Arctan swallowed.

Hard.

“He never lets anyone escape,” Aurelion continued. “Even I… was fortunate to survive my encounter with him.”

The boy closed his eyes.

His jaw set.

His faith did not break.

It folded tighter.

Harder.

Into something colder.

Far from the flames — beyond the sirens — beyond the reach of scanners and drones —

A lone figure moved through the dark.

Slowly now.

Not a phantom.

Not unstoppable.

His steps dragged slightly.

His breath strained.

Beneath his coat, blood crept down his side — hidden, but real.

He slipped into the belly of an empty storage yard and sank against a rusted container, letting the cold metal bleed the heat from his skin.

The world spun.

He waited.

Minutes.

Maybe hours.

Until the burning in his muscles ebbed.

Until the pain dulled enough to stand.

He did.

He walked.

Not toward allies.

Not toward rebels.

Toward a dim shop wedged between abandoned freight offices — lights low, windows dusty, a crooked lantern hanging over the door.

He knocked twice.

The door opened.

An old merchant stood there — face weathered, eyes tired — and when he saw the man in the doorway, he did not scream or run or call the authorities.

He sighed.

“You again,” he said quietly.

The Ghost lowered his hood.

They looked at each other for a long moment.

Old familiarity.

Old history.

“…You’re hurt,” the merchant murmured.

The Ghost did not deny it.

They stepped inside.

The door closed.

And the night outside went on pretending it knew who the hero was.
