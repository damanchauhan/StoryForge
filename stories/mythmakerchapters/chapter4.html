The Mythmaker

Chapter IV — Resolve

Before the uniform, before the scars time hid beneath discipline, before his voice learned to steady itself in command—

Arctan Valeon had been a boy who laughed easily.

He grew up in a small district near the inner ring, where the buildings were tall but the streets still felt like neighborhoods. His parents ran a quiet tailor shop, its windows always warm with yellow light and lined with jackets, uniforms, and pressed shirts that smelled faintly of soap and clean thread.

His mother sang while she worked.

His father taught him how to measure cloth with careful hands.

Evening meals were always shared — no matter how long the day had been. They sat together at a narrow wooden table, steam rising from bowls, the hum of the city drifting

His father would say:

“Pride is not shouting, Arctan. Pride is living with dignity.”

His mother would smile and brush his hair back:

“And dignity means kindness first.”

He believed the world was gentle because they were.

He remembered walking with them on sunny mornings when his mother wore her red scarf and his father teased him for walking too fast. He remembered weekends at the central gardens, the smell of fresh bread from corner vendors, his mother taking his arm as if time itself could never carry them away from each other.

He remembered feeling safe.

He remembered thinking the world could not change.

He remembered the day it did.

It was late afternoon — the sky a pale, washed-out gold.

His parents had gone to the Central Trust Tower to finalize a loan agreement for expanding the shop. Arctan stayed behind at a friend’s apartment, sprawled on the floor with a deck of cards, laughing over nothing and everything.

The television was on in the background.

The laughter stopped when the broadcast cut to emergency coverage.

A pillar of smoke rose from the heart of the city — thick, black, wrong. The reporter’s voice trembled as words fell out faster than sense could shape them.

Explosion — Central Trust Tower — mass casualties — emergency response en route—

Arctan sat upright.

His friend said his name.

He didn’t hear it.

The screen zoomed in.

Broken glass raining like dust.

A building like a ribcage torn open.

He whispered,

“…no…”

His hands went cold.

He called.

And called.

And called.

No one answered.

Hours blurred into noise — sirens, crowds, choked voices speaking into radios. The world narrowed to hospital corridors, smoke-soaked blankets, officials with tired eyes.

A quiet room.

A folded paper.

A name spelled out too cleanly to feel real.

They did not let him see the bodies.

They told him it was better that way.

Better.

The city lights outside the window looked like teeth.

Days later, when grief had dried into something heavy and numbing, a man in uniform sat across from him in a small, sterile office.

The officer did not rush his words.

He spoke carefully — like he was delivering a verdict already carved in stone.

“The investigation has concluded,” he said.

Arctan stared through him.

“The attack was carried out by the rebel known as the Ghost.”

Silence swallowed the room.

Hundreds dead.

Civilians.

Workers.

Families.

His parents.

His world.

All taken by a name.

A shadow.

A story whispered in fear.

The Ghost.

The word burned through him like a brand pressed against the heart.

Something inside him cracked —

— and hardened.

Not into hatred at first.

Into resolve.

Into the need for meaning.

Into the need for justice.

Into the need for someone to answer.

He joined the academy the first year he was old enough.

He ran until his lungs tore, studied until dawn, fought until every bruise became a promise. He learned to control his breathing, his temper, his trembling hands. He learned discipline like a prayer spoken silently to his past.

Some whispered he pushed himself too hard.

Some admired him for it.

He didn’t care either way.

He wasn’t there to be admired.

He was there to make sure what happened to him never happened again.

That was where Aurelion Vex found him.

The Executioner did not speak to him like a superior speaking to a recruit.

He spoke to him like a man who understood loss.

He read Arctan’s file.

He looked him in the eye.

And instead of pity — he offered purpose.

“You have potential,” Aurelion said. “Raw, but resolute. You seek justice — not rage. That is rare.”

Arctan bowed his head.

“I want to stop monsters,” he said quietly.

Aurelion placed a hand on his shoulder.

“Then I will teach you how.”

And he did.

He trained Arctan personally — long hours in silent halls, tactical drills, strategy maps that looked more like chess boards than battle plans. He spoke of order. Of unity. Of sacrifice.

He taught him when to act.

He taught him when to wait.

He taught him the cost of hesitation — and the cost of needless blood.

And sometimes, when the training was over, they would sit beneath the academy’s high stone arches and simply talk — about the nation, the future, responsibility.

About pride.

About strength without cruelty.

Arctan felt, for the first time since the tower fell, that the world might be whole again.

That he could become more than grief stitched together into a man’s shape.

That he could stand between others and the kind of loss that had devoured his childhood.

That he could hunt the Ghost

— and finally, finally

lay the past to rest.

The lantern burned low inside the merchant’s shop, its weak amber glow settling into the cracks of the old wooden counter and the shelves lined with rusted tools, salvaged parts, and silent machines that no longer served any world that still pretended to be alive.

The merchant worked quietly.

His hands were steady, but his eyes weren’t.

He knelt beside the Ghost and pressed a cloth to the wound beneath the coat. Blood spread through the fabric slowly — dark, warm, stubborn.

“You shouldn’t keep doing this,” the merchant muttered, voice lined with years of tired worry. “You’re tearing yourself apart.”

The Ghost did not respond.

His breath was slow, controlled.

Too controlled.

The merchant stitched the torn flesh, thread pulling skin together with small, ugly bites.

“I heard the broadcasts,” he said softly. “Half a division is dead. Again. They’re saying the streets will go into mourning.”

He swallowed.

“They’re saying you did it.”

Silence lingered like smoke.

The Ghost’s eyes were shadowed beneath the hood — the light never quite settling into them, as though something inside refused to reflect anything back.

“…You didn’t have to kill them all,” the merchant whispered. “Some of them… some of them were just doing their jobs.”

The Ghost exhaled — not in pain.

In irritation.

“They chose their side,” he said.

His voice was low and cold — stripped of warmth, stripped of hesitation.

The merchant looked at him — at the hands that had ended lives without leaving echoes behind, at the face that never trembled after a night of blood.

“You’ve changed,” the merchant said. “You weren’t always like this.”

The Ghost’s lips pulled into something that might once have been a smile — now twisted, humorless, empty of anything gentle.

“No,” he replied quietly. “I just stopped pretending.”

The merchant set the needle aside.

He hesitated.

Then finally asked the question that had haunted him for years.

“…Why?” he said. “Why do you keep killing? Why burn the city? Why tear at everything like this?”

The Ghost’s fingers tightened against the edge of the table — veins standing out pale beneath his skin.

He lifted his head.

And when he spoke, there was nothing human in the words.

“I will kill them all.”

His voice didn’t shake.

It didn’t rise.

It settled.

Like ash.

“I will burn this hell,” he continued, eyes glittering with something cruel and absolute. “I will rip it apart, tear it down piece by piece until nothing stands. If no one else will do it — I will.”

The merchant stepped back — instinctively — as though something in the air had turned sharp.

The Ghost’s breathing steadied further, as if the promise itself soothed him.

“I will hunt every last one of them,” he said. “Every soldier. Every loyal dog. Every obedient little worshiper of that self-crowned god.”

His jaw clenched.

“I will kill that bastard,” he hissed. “And if the world has to burn with him…”

His eyes glowed faintly in the lantern light.

“…then it burns.”

The room felt smaller.

Colder.

For a moment — the merchant did not see a wounded man.

He saw a storm wearing a body.

He saw a void that had chosen to walk.

He swallowed.

“You’re… becoming a monster,” he said quietly.

The Ghost didn’t deny it.

He didn’t even blink.

“If that’s what it takes,” he replied,

“…then let me be one.”

The lantern flickered.

Outside, the night pressed against the glass.

And somewhere far away — the city slept under the shadow of a lie…

…while the thing they called the Ghost dreamed of fire.
