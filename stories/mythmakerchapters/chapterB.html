The Mythmaker

Bonus — The Story of the Monster and the Hero

Before there was a Hero, there was only a boy.

His name was Aurelion — son of a quiet man with a quiet life, the kind of father who believed the world functioned because ordinary people held it together. Aurelion grew up on discipline, humility, and the belief that service meant honor.

So he joined the military.

He learned command.

He learned strategy.

He learned how easily men followed those who sounded certain.

Then one winter evening, the news reached him:

His father had been executed.

The charge was treason.

They said he had jeopardized national security.

They said he had aided rebels.

They said many things.

Aurelion did not believe any of them.

He dug through records. Reports. Transcripts. Classified memos buried under meaningless language. Every answer led to silence — until he found what the official files buried:

His father had been in the wrong place when a powerful official needed someone expendable.

A scapegoat.

A body to hang guilt on so that others could walk away untouched.

Aurelion did not grieve after that.

He made a decision.

If the world needed villains…

then he would choose who they were.

The first man he killed was the one who had condemned his father.

The nation called it justice.

The people called it courage.

They called him a Hero.

And Aurelion learned something important:

It was not enough to be righteous.

It was far more powerful to be necessary.

He rose through politics.

Through charisma.

Through blood no one ever traced back to him.

He staged a coup and wrapped it in the language of salvation.

He became a ruler who fought for his citizens.

Or so the world believed.

No one questioned why every enemy he defeated appeared at the perfect time.

No one questioned why every “threat” collapsed into legend the moment he touched it.

Aurelion discovered he liked the feeling — the adoration, the worship, the faith that made him untouchable.

So he turned necessity into a system.

He made villains.

One by one.

He hunted them.

Destroyed them.

And every victory carved his name deeper into the nation’s spine — until even if he spoke the truth aloud, no one would believe it.

They would call it another lie told by a lesser man.

And one night…

he saw a petty thief running through an alley — breathless, frightened, desperate.

A scuffle.

A frightened cop.

More arriving.

The boy fought to escape — clumsy, terrified, human.

Aurelion watched from the shadows.

Then stepped in.

He shot every officer.

Clean.

Efficient.

Quiet.

He erased the cameras.

He rewrote the scene.

The next morning, the world saw a headline:

GHOST KILLS POLICE — VANISHES INTO THE DARK

A faceless monster.

A perfect myth.

A symbol he could shape and break whenever he pleased.

Aurelion smiled.

And somewhere far below the polished towers and shining streets…

a life unravelled.

The man who would become the Ghost had once simply been a young man behind on rent.

He had searched for work.

He had been turned away.

A debt collector made him an offer:

One small theft.

A convenience store.

No one harmed.

A foolish mistake wrapped in desperation.

He told himself he’d repay the owner later.

Then a cop caught him.

The baton struck without warning — panic, instinct, fear colliding at once. He fought back. More officers came. He fled in terror.

He thought it was over.

The next morning, he was everywhere.

They called him a murderer.

A cop-killer.

A traitor.

He ran to his friend’s home.

His friend was dead.

He ran to his family.

Dead, too.

Everyone he knew — every face that could have defended him — erased.

Only his name remained.

And the lie.

Months passed.

He hid in alleys and abandoned rooms, hunted and starving — but still alive.

Aurelion grew bored.

So the world required drama again.

One afternoon, the Ghost went to the bank to withdraw what little money he had left — a final attempt to run somewhere no one followed him.

A bright flash tore the sky open.

The building collapsed.

Hundreds died.

He crawled out burned and bleeding — alive by accident.

By nightfall, the news had already decided:

GHOST KILLS CIVILIANS IN BOMBING MASSACRE

He begged reality to make sense.

It didn’t.

He fought back because surviving meant fighting.

And every time he spared someone…

…the reports claimed he slaughtered them anyway.

Truth was not part of the story.

Only narrative.

Only usefulness.

Eventually, one night, his wounds failed him.

He collapsed in an alley, expecting the end.

When he opened his eyes, he was lying on a clean floor beneath the scent of tea and worn wood.

An old man hummed while boiling water.

Not afraid.

Not cruel.

Not armed.

“You’re awake,” the man said softly. “Good. I found you before the rain chilled you to death.”

The Ghost flinched — rigid, cornered.

The old man lifted his hands calmly.

“If I wanted you gone,” he said gently, “you wouldn’t have woken up.”

He treated the wounds.

He spoke kindly.

He saw A human being, not a headline.

“You know who I am,” the Ghost whispered.

“Yes,” the man replied. “But tonight, you are simply… someone who needed help.”

The Ghost never stayed long.

He never brought trouble to his door.

But in a world that had carved his existence into a hunt…

…the old man became the closest thing to home.

A fragile kindness in a life built from fear.

And far above them both

Aurelion Vex continued building his legend.

He funded secret technologies only he was allowed to use.

He created task forces and wars to justify power.

He shaped soldiers into symbols — young, bright, devoted.

When he found a promising boy — disciplined, grieving, loyal —

he did not yet decide whether to mold him into a successor…

…or into the next villain.

Either way,

the role would be useful.

And so the world was given its myth:

A shining hero who saved the nation.

A monster haunting its shadows.

And a truth

buried alive

between them.
