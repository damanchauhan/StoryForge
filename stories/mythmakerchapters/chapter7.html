The Mythmaker

Chapter VII — Peek Into the Abyss

🏠 StoryForge

For so long, the story had been told in one direction.

A Hero.

A Monster.

A nation that believed every word spoken to it.

The narrator’s voice slid back into the world like a knife slipping between ribs.

Isn’t it arrogant… to assume the narrator cannot lie? Even gods twist the truth — sometimes for order… sometimes for pleasure.

So let us stop listening to the light… and peer into the abyss ourselves.

The story rewound.

The dust.

The broken windows.

The room where the fight had already been written into legend.

Arctan’s fists crashed again and again into the Ghost’s face — blood splattering across the cracked tiles, breath ragged, grief boiling into violence.

The Ghost did not scream.

He laughed once — dry, hollow, broken.

Then he spoke.

His voice was frayed… but clear.

“Your family,” he said quietly. “Tell me… were they soldiers?”

Arctan froze for a fraction of a second.

“No,” he snarled. “They were civilians.”

A pause.

A breath.

The Ghost’s eyes sharpened — cold… furious.

“I never killed civilians,” he whispered. “Not until today.”

Arctan blinked.

His grip faltered.

The Ghost’s head turned — not toward the ceiling…

…but toward Aurelion.

“Why don’t you ask,” he said —

“…your oh-so-dear hero?”

Arctan slowly turned.

Aurelion did not move.

He watched them both like a man measuring the distance between two graves.

Arctan’s voice cracked.

“You… knew him?”

Aurelion exhaled — calm, unhurried — as if the truth had never once frightened him.

“You were far above my expectations,” he said softly. “Ghost… or should I say—”

He spoke a name.

A warm name.

A human name.

A name that didn’t belong to a monster — but to a boy who once believed in heroes.

Arctan’s stomach twisted.

“…You knew him?” he whispered. “You knew — and you never told anyone? Why did you lie to me?”

Aurelion smiled faintly.

Not kind.

Not cruel.

Simply… superior.

“Why,” he said,

“…listen to a dead man?”

The Ghost’s chest heaved — fury erupting from years of hunted silence.

“You chased me for YEARS!” he roared. “Why? WHY? WHAT DID I DO TO YOU?!”

His voice cracked into agony.

“I worshipped you — like every other idiot in this country! I believed in you! I trusted you!”

His hand slammed against the floor.

“And one day I wake up a murderer — a ghost — and the whole world wants me dead!”

His scream tore through the room.

Aurelion’s eyes glinted.

“Every hero,” he said calmly,

“…needs a villain.”

The words landed like bullets.

“You were there,” he continued. “Wrong place. Wrong time. Convenient. I turned a petty thief into a myth… and the nation needed myths.”

He tilted his head.

“You should be grateful.”

The Ghost’s laugh broke into a sob.

“I had to hunt people,” he whispered. “To eat. To survive. You turned every road into a noose around my neck.”

Arctan staggered backward.

The room felt colder.

His world — the one built on order, faith, and righteousness —

fractured.

He looked at Aurelion.

He didn’t recognize him anymore.

Aurelion stepped forward — slow, deliberate — toward the Ghost.

The Ghost’s fingers trembled.

Then tightened.

“I’ll take you with me,” he hissed.

He opened his hand.

A grenade pin clattered across the floor.

Arctan’s eyes widened.

“NO!”

He shoved Aurelion back — instinct, loyalty, confusion — all of it crashing at once.

The world flashed white.

The sound swallowed everything.

Stone. Dust. Bone.

Silence.

Smoke.

When it cleared…

The Ghost lay still.

Arctan lay beside him — unmoving.

Broken.

Aurelion staggered to his feet — unharmed beneath a shimmering shield of pale light, fading from around him like mist.

He looked down at the bodies.

Not mournful.

Not shaken.

Just calculating.

“…What a fool,” he muttered softly.

He touched the shallow wound along his side — cosmetic, intentional.

“He thought a scratch meant he could kill me.”

He glanced at Arctan — his student — the last witness.

“…And you,” he whispered under his breath, voice almost amused,

“had to die. It saves me… so much trouble.”

His coat straightened.

His breath steadied.

His face rewrote itself into grief.

Footsteps thundered up the stairs.

Soldiers burst in — gasping — reaching out to hold him upright.

“Sir—!”

Aurelion winced.

Played weak.

Played wounded.

“Only… I survived…”

His voice broke on cue.

“…the Ghost… is dead.”

They believed him.

They always did.

And outside…

the nation prepared to celebrate

the story they had been given.

Because the truth

had died

on that floor.
