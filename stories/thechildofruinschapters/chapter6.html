The Child of Ruin

Chapter 6: The First Hunt

Time passed quietly upon the mountain.

Seasons came and went.

Snow melted.

Flowers bloomed.

Leaves fell.

And before either of them truly noticed, Rowan had grown.


The tiny child Aldric once carried in his arms now ran across the mountain trails with boundless energy.

His legs had grown longer.

His voice louder.

His curiosity infinitely more dangerous.

Some things, however, never changed.

Especially the wooden sword.

“Hyah!”

The sword struck a tree.

“Hyah!”

It struck the tree again.

“Hyah!”

A third strike landed.

Then a loud crack echoed through the clearing.


Silence followed.

Rowan froze.

Slowly, he looked down.

The wooden blade had snapped in half.

For a long moment, neither piece moved.

Then Rowan carefully picked them up.

Trying to fit them back together.

The broken ends refused.

Again.

And again.

Still nothing.

The boy’s shoulders slowly drooped.


Across the clearing, Aldric watched.

He said nothing.

The old man simply returned to splitting firewood.

Pretending not to notice.

That evening, Rowan sat unusually quietly.

The broken sword rested beside him.

Occasionally he glanced at it.

Then looked away.

Then glanced back again.

Aldric hid a smile.

The sword had survived years of abuse.

Truthfully, he was surprised it had lasted this long.


When Rowan finally fell asleep, the old man picked up the broken weapon.

His thumb traced the worn handle.

Smooth.

Polished by countless small hands.

The corners of his lips rose.

Then he reached for a piece of wood.

The next morning began with shouting.

“Grandpa!”

Aldric winced.

There was no louder force in nature than an excited child.

The old man stepped outside.


Rowan stood waiting.

A wooden sword hanging from his waist.

New.

Longer.

Better balanced.

Still clearly handmade.

The boy’s eyes widened.

For a moment he simply stared.

Then:

“Sword!”

Aldric laughed.

“Yes.”

Rowan immediately grabbed it.

Turning it over.

Inspecting every inch.

As though it were a priceless treasure.


A few moments later he threw himself at Aldric.

The old man barely caught him.

“Careful!”

Rowan only grinned.

The sword remained clutched tightly in his hands.

A few days later, Rowan decided to ask a question.

One he had apparently been saving.

“Grandpa.”

“Hm?”

“When can I hunt?”

Aldric immediately regretted hearing the question.

“No.”

“Why?”

“Because you’re six.”

“I’m big six.”

“That’s not how that works.”


Rowan crossed his arms.

Aldric ignored him.

The boy continued staring.

Aldric continued ignoring him.

The staring intensified.

The old man sighed.

This child was relentless.

Three days later, Rowan somehow managed to appear beside him carrying a backpack.

A backpack nearly larger than himself.

“Ready.”

Aldric stared.

The child stared back.

The old man rubbed his face.

Defeat tasted familiar.

“Fine.”

Rowan cheered.

The mountain echoed with victory.

The hunt began before sunrise.

For the first hour, Rowan talked constantly.

For the second hour, he asked questions constantly.

By the third hour, he was tired.

Aldric found this deeply satisfying.


The boy sat upon a rock.

Breathing heavily.

“This is hard.”

Aldric nodded.

“Hunting usually is.”

Rowan frowned.

He had expected something much more heroic.

Eventually they found tracks.

Rabbit tracks.

Fresh ones.

Aldric knelt beside them.

“See these?”

Rowan nodded.

The old man pointed.

“Follow them.”

The boy’s eyes lit up.

For the next half hour, he treated the task as the greatest adventure ever conceived.

Then he saw it.

A rabbit.

Small.

Brown.

Nibbling peacefully on grass.

Rowan froze.

The rabbit froze.

The entire world froze.


Slowly, Rowan reached for a stone.

Aldric watched silently.

The throw was terrible.

Absolutely terrible.

Yet by some miracle, it struck.

The rabbit collapsed.

Rowan stared.

The rabbit remained very still.

Silence followed.

Then:

“I got it!”

The boy exploded with excitement.

Aldric couldn’t help laughing.

“Looks like you did.”
