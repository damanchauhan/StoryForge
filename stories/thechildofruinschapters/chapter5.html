The Child of Ruin

Chapter 5: Grandpa’s Sword

Spring arrived quietly.

The snow covering the mountains slowly disappeared.

Green returned to the forests.

Flowers began blooming along the hillsides.

And Rowan grew.


It happened so gradually that Aldric hardly noticed.

Until one morning when the boy tried squeezing into a shirt that no longer fit.

The result was ten minutes of struggling, one torn sleeve, and a very angry Rowan.

Aldric laughed so hard he nearly fell out of his chair.

Rowan did not appreciate it.

Not even a little.

A year had passed since the day Aldric found him.

The quiet child who once communicated through cries and confused noises had become something far more dangerous.

A curious child.

Which, Aldric was learning, was much harder to survive.


“Grandpa!”

The shout echoed across the mountain.

Aldric froze.

Even after hearing it dozens of times, the word still felt strange.

Warm.

Unexpected.

The old man turned.

A blur shot toward him.

A second later, Rowan crashed directly into his legs.

The impact nearly knocked the boy over.

Rowan recovered immediately.

Pretending nothing had happened.

Aldric raised an eyebrow.

The child looked away.

Clearly innocent.

The old man laughed.

“Of course.”

Life settled into a comfortable routine.

Aldric hunted.

Gathered supplies.

Maintained the cabin.

Rowan helped.

Or at least he claimed he was helping.

In reality, most of his contributions created additional work.


One afternoon, Rowan proudly carried a bundle of sticks toward the cabin.

A few moments later, Aldric discovered he had accidentally brought back half a bush.

The boy remained very proud of himself.

Aldric decided not to mention it.

The obsession began shortly afterward.

Aldric returned from hunting one evening carrying a rabbit over his shoulder.

The moment Rowan saw him, his eyes lit up.

Not because of the rabbit.

Because of the sword.

The weapon hung at Aldric’s waist.

Simple.

Old.

Worn from years of use.

Rowan immediately pointed.

“Sword!”

Aldric sighed.

The boy had learned a new word.

Unfortunately, it was that one.

Over the following weeks, the situation worsened.

Everything became a sword.

A stick?

Sword.

A branch?

Sword.

A spoon?

Apparently also a sword.

One afternoon Aldric discovered Rowan attempting to fight a tree with a piece of firewood.

The tree won.

Decisively.

Rowan remained determined.

One evening, Aldric sat outside sharpening a knife.

Nearby, Rowan swung a crooked stick with all the grace of a falling rock.

“Hyah!”

The stick struck a bush.

“Hyah!”

It struck the ground.

“Hyah!”

The stick snapped in half.

Rowan froze.

Silence followed.

The boy stared at the broken stick.

The broken stick stared back.

Slowly, Rowan’s shoulders slumped.

Aldric pretended not to notice.

The child looked genuinely heartbroken.

The old man hid a smile.

That night, after Rowan had fallen asleep, Aldric remained awake.

A small piece of wood rested in his hands.

The cabin was quiet except for the crackling of the fire.

Slowly, carefully, he carved.

Hours passed.

Shavings gathered around his chair.

By the time he finished, dawn was approaching.

The wooden sword wasn’t perfect.

Far from it.

The blade was slightly crooked.

The handle uneven.

A craftsman would probably cry if they saw it.

Aldric smiled anyway.

It would do.

The next morning, Rowan woke to find something resting beside his bed.

The boy blinked.

Then blinked again.

A wooden sword.

His eyes widened.

He immediately grabbed it.

The weapon was small enough for him to hold comfortably.

Light.

Simple.

Perfect.

“Sword!”

Aldric winced.

Not even a good morning.

The child jumped off the bed and sprinted toward him.

“Grandpa!”

Then raised the wooden sword proudly.

“Sword!”

Aldric laughed.

“Yes.”

The old man nodded.

“It’s a sword.”
