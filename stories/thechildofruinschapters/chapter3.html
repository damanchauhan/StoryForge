The Child of Ruin

Chapter 3: Rowan

The next few days passed quietly.

Snow continued to fall outside the cabin.

The mountains remained peaceful.

And for the first time in a long while, the old man found himself adjusting to another presence in his home.

It wasn’t easy.

The child seemed determined to make everything more difficult.


The old man was preparing breakfast when he heard a loud thump from behind him.

He turned.

The child sat on the floor.

Blinking.

Apparently confused by how he had gotten there.

The old man stared.

The child stared back.

Several moments passed.

Then the child pointed accusingly at the floor.

The old man burst out laughing.


“You fell.”

The child frowned.

The old man’s laughter only grew louder.

The child looked deeply offended.

As though the floor had personally betrayed him.

“Don’t look at me.”

The old man wiped a tear from his eye.

“You were the one trying to walk.”

The child crossed his arms.

The old man laughed even harder.

Later that day, the child followed him around the cabin.

Not intentionally.

At least, that’s what the old man told himself.

Yet every time he turned around, the child was there.

If he walked to the fireplace, the child followed.

If he walked to the table, the child followed.

If he stepped outside, the child sat by the doorway and watched.


The old man eventually sighed.

“You know.”

The child looked up.

“This is becoming a problem.”

The child blinked.

The old man pointed toward himself.

“You keep following me.”

The child continued blinking.

Then crawled closer.

The old man stared.

The child stared back.

The old man groaned.

“Wonderful.”


The child smiled.

The old man immediately looked away.

“…Don’t do that.”

The smile widened.

The old man covered his face with one hand.

The little brat was weaponizing cuteness.

The cabin slowly changed.

The old man noticed it one morning.

A wooden cup sat in a place where it normally didn’t.

A blanket had somehow migrated across the room.

Tiny footprints appeared everywhere.

The silence was gone.

Not entirely.

But enough.

The cabin no longer felt empty.

The realization was strange.

The old man wasn’t sure whether he liked it.


A few seconds later, the child tripped over his own blanket.

The old man sighed.

He definitely liked it.

The child was sitting near the fireplace one evening when the old man suddenly spoke.

“You know.”

The child looked up.

“I’ve been calling you kid.”

The child blinked.

“And boy.”

Another blink.

The old man nodded thoughtfully.

“That’s not very creative.”

The child continued staring.


The old man stared into the fire.

A long silence followed.

The flames crackled softly.

Snow drifted past the windows.

The child yawned.

The old man’s gaze wandered outside.

Toward the tree standing beside the cabin.

Its branches swayed gently beneath the weight of snow.

Strong.

Steady.

Unmoving despite the cold mountain winds.


The old man watched it for a moment.

Then smiled.

“Rowan.”

The word slipped from his mouth naturally.

The child tilted his head.

The old man looked at him.

“Rowan.”

The child blinked.

The old man chuckled.

“Yeah.”

He nodded to himself.

“That sounds right.”


The child crawled closer.

The old man absentmindedly rested a hand on his head.

“Every child deserves a name.”

His voice was quieter now.

Almost thoughtful.

The child obviously didn’t understand.

The old man knew that.

Still, he continued speaking.

Perhaps more to himself than anyone else.

“Can’t spend your whole life being called kid.”

The child grabbed his sleeve.

The old man froze.


For a moment, neither moved.

Then the child rested his head against the old man’s side.

The old man’s expression softened.

A strange warmth spread through his chest.

One he hadn’t felt in a very long time.

A dangerous warmth.

The kind that made people care.

The kind that made people hope.

The kind that made people afraid of losing something.


The old man gently patted the child’s head.

“Rowan.”

The child looked up.

The old man pointed at him.

“Rowan.”

The child stared.

The old man repeated it again.

And again.

And again.

Until eventually the child pointed at himself.

The old man’s eyes widened.

Then he laughed.

A genuine laugh.

Warm.

Unrestrained.


“That’s right.”

He ruffled the child’s hair.

“You’re Rowan.”

The child smiled.

A small smile.

Barely visible.

Yet somehow it filled the entire room.

The old man’s laughter faded.

For a moment, he simply looked at the child.

At Rowan.

Then a quiet smile appeared on his weathered face.

“Welcome home, Rowan.”


Outside, snow continued to fall.

Inside, the cabin was warm.

And for the first time since he had been found beneath the ruins…

The child had a name.
